\begin{abstract}
\textbf{Background:} Variable selection for causal inference from observational biomedical data is challenging, as overlooking confounders or conditioning on colliders leads to biased estimates. While vast causal knowledge exists in biomedical literature, manually extracting this information for principled variable selection is impractical at scale.

\textbf{Methods:} We developed CausalKnowledgeTrace, a Python-based computational framework with Django web interface that systematically leverages structured causal knowledge from the Semantic MEDLINE Database (SemMedDB) to inform variable selection in causal studies. The system implements a six-stage analysis pipeline using NetworkX for graph operations, including graph parsing, basic analysis, comprehensive cycle detection, systematic generic node removal, post-removal analysis, and formal causal inference with bias detection.

\textbf{Results:} Analysis of the hypertension-Alzheimer's relationship across three degree neighborhoods (1-3) demonstrated systematic scaling of causal complexity: 361-866 variables, 429-1,442 relationships, with graph densities of 0.0033-0.0019. The analysis revealed complex cyclic structures with 54-606 baseline cycles across degree levels. Processing times ranged from 0.3-1.0 seconds for all three degrees, demonstrating computational efficiency for complex biomedical networks. Key confounders identified across all degrees included inflammation, diabetes, insulin resistance, obesity, and ischemia.

\textbf{Conclusions:} CausalKnowledgeTrace provides a scalable, evidence-based approach to causal graph construction that systematically identifies confounders and bias structures often missed by conventional approaches. The Python-Django architecture enables both standalone analysis and integration into larger computational workflows, representing a significant advance in computational support for causal inference in biomedical research.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Statement of Significance}

\noindent\textbf{Problem or Issue}\\
Selecting proper confounders and variables for causal inference from observational biomedical datasets is challenging and often biased by limited expertise or manual review.

\noindent\textbf{What is Already Known}\\
Existing approaches rely on domain experts, statistical variable screening, or manual construction of causal graphs, but these often overlook literature-documented confounders and complex biases.

\noindent\textbf{What this Paper Adds}\\
This paper introduces an automated, literature-based framework for synthesizing and validating causal graphs, identifying critical variables and complex bias structures, such as M-bias and butterfly bias, with full evidentiary traceability.

\noindent\textbf{Who would benefit from the new knowledge in this paper?}\\
Epidemiologists, biomedical researchers, informaticians, and clinical investigators seeking reliable and transparent causal modeling for observational studies.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Causal inference from observational data has become increasingly critical in biomedical research as the scale and complexity of available datasets continue to expand. \cite{hernan2020causal} Electronic health records, genomic databases, and large-scale epidemiological studies offer unprecedented opportunities to understand disease mechanisms, evaluate therapeutic interventions, and inform clinical decision-making. \cite{hernan2016using} However, the validity of causal conclusions drawn from these observational data sources fundamentally depends on appropriate control for confounding variables—factors that influence both the exposure and the outcome of interest. \cite{pearl2009causality}

The challenge of identifying appropriate confounding variables has intensified as biomedical research has evolved from small, focused studies to large-scale analyses involving hundreds of potential covariates. Traditional approaches to variable selection rely heavily on domain expertise, statistical screening methods, or manual literature review, each carrying significant limitations.\cite{shrier2008reducing} Domain experts may overlook confounders documented in adjacent fields or emerging research areas. Statistical variable selection methods cannot distinguish between confounders, mediators, and colliders without causal assumptions, potentially leading to biased estimates. Manual literature review becomes increasingly impractical as PubMed now contains over 35 million citations and adds approximately 1.5 million new articles annually.

Recent advances in causal inference methodology have emphasized the importance of directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) for representing causal assumptions and identifying appropriate adjustment sets. \cite{pearl2009causality, shrier2008reducing} DAG-based approaches offer a principled framework for distinguishing between different types of bias and determining the minimal sufficient sets of variables required for confounding control \cite{textor2016robust}. However, constructing realistic DAGs for complex biomedical phenomena remains challenging when attempting to incorporate the breadth of relevant causal knowledge documented in the scientific literature. This challenge is compounded by four fundamental barriers: overwhelming literature volume, inconsistent terminology across publications, ambiguous directionality of reported relationships, and lack of systematic approaches for integrating literature-derived knowledge into formal causal models.

To address these barriers, we require computational approaches that can automatically extract, validate, and synthesize causal knowledge from large-scale literature repositories. The Semantic MEDLINE Database (SemMedDB) provides a foundation for such approaches, containing over 100 million semantic predications extracted from PubMed abstracts using the SemRep natural language processing system. \cite{kilicoglu2012semmeddb} These structured subject-predicate-object triples capture causal, associational, and mechanistic relationships between biomedical concepts. Unlike manually curated databases with limited coverage, SemMedDB offers comprehensive scope across biomedical domains while maintaining a structured format suitable for automated processing.

However, raw extraction from SemMedDB presents challenges including low-quality predications, contradictory relationships, and terminological inconsistencies requiring systematic filtering and validation. Recent developments in large language models offer promising solutions for enhancing the quality and interpretation of literature-derived causal knowledge. \cite{thirunavukarasu2023large}

Building on these capabilities, we developed CausalKnowledgeTrace to systematically address each barrier to literature-based variable selection through automated extraction, multi-stage quality filtering, semantic consolidation, large language model-based validation, and systematic bias detection. \cite{ding2015adjust}

The primary objectives of this work are threefold. First, we develop a scalable computational framework for constructing literature-derived directed acyclic graphs that systematically inform variable selection in causal studies while maintaining complete transparency about evidentiary basis. Second, we create user-friendly interfaces and standardized output formats that facilitate seamless integration of literature-derived causal knowledge into existing epidemiological workflows. Third, we demonstrate the practical utility and validation of this approach across diverse biomedical research contexts, with particular focus on identifying confounders, mediators, and bias patterns that might be overlooked through traditional manual approaches.

The remainder of this paper describes the technical implementation of CausalKnowledgeTrace, evaluates its performance through comprehensive comparison with expert judgment and systematic literature review, and discusses the implications for evidence-based causal inference practice in biomedical research.

\subsection{System Architecture and Pipeline Overview}

CausalKnowledgeTrace operates as a modular computational framework integrating Python-based analysis components with a Django web application interface. The system follows a six-stage pipeline implemented in Python using NetworkX for graph operations and comprehensive CSV-based output for reproducibility and validation.

\subsection{Knowledge Source and Data Integration}

The system utilizes the Semantic MEDLINE Database (SemMedDB) as its primary knowledge source, containing structured semantic predications extracted from PubMed abstracts. Graph construction leverages pre-generated causal assertion files in JSON format, containing directed relationships between biomedical concepts identified through literature mining. Each relationship includes supporting evidence with PubMed identifiers (PMIDs) and citation counts.

\subsection{Six-Stage Analysis Pipeline}

The core analysis pipeline consists of six sequential stages, each implemented as independent Python modules with standardized input/output interfaces:

\textbf{Stage 1 (Graph Parsing):} Converts JSON causal assertions into NetworkX directed graphs, extracting node and edge metadata including predicates and evidence counts. Outputs include serialized GraphML files and comprehensive edge lists with provenance information.

\textbf{Stage 2 (Basic Analysis):} Computes fundamental graph statistics including node degrees, centrality measures (betweenness, closeness), strongly connected component identification, and initial cycle detection. Generates detailed node-level metrics and graph-wide summary statistics.

\textbf{Stage 3 (Cycle Analysis):} Performs comprehensive cycle enumeration using Johnson's algorithm within strongly connected components. Tracks per-node cycle participation counts, cycle length distributions, and samples representative cycles for detailed analysis.

\textbf{Stage 4 (Node Removal):} Implements systematic removal of generic biomedical terms (e.g., "Disease", "Symptoms", "Patients") identified through centrality analysis and expert curation. Measures cycle reduction impact for individual and combined node removals, generating reduced graphs for downstream analysis.

\textbf{Stage 5 (Post-Removal Analysis):} Analyzes residual graph structure after generic node removal, identifying remaining problematic nodes and quantifying cycle reduction effectiveness. Provides recommendations for additional node removal if DAG requirements are not met.

\textbf{Stage 6 (Causal Inference):} Applies formal causal inference methods on DAG-compliant graphs, identifying minimally sufficient adjustment sets using the backdoor criterion and detecting potential instrumental variables. Includes bias structure analysis for complex confounding scenarios.

\subsection{Graph Post-Processing and Quality Control}

\subsubsection{Centrality-Based Graph Pruning and Refinement}

The initial graphs contain high-degree hub nodes representing both specific biomedical concepts and generic terms that introduce artifactual relationships. Our Python pipeline implements systematic identification and removal of these problematic nodes through combined centrality analysis and expert curation.

Using NetworkX, we compute comprehensive centrality metrics including in-degree, out-degree, total degree, and betweenness centrality for all nodes. Betweenness centrality quantifies the number of shortest paths passing through each node, identifying structural bottlenecks in the causal network. High-centrality nodes undergo expert review to distinguish between clinically meaningful hubs and generic biomedical terms.

The system maintains a curated list of 9 generic biomedical terms including Disease, Symptoms, Functional disorder, Syndrome, Complication, Diagnosis, Obstruction, Physical findings, and Adverse effects. Stage 4 of the pipeline systematically removes these nodes and measures the impact on cycle reduction.

The pipeline generates detailed impact assessments for individual node removals, ranking nodes by cycle reduction effectiveness. This approach enables researchers to understand which generic terms most significantly contribute to spurious cyclic relationships in their specific research context.

\subsubsection{Confounder Identification and Bias Analysis}

The pipeline implements systematic confounder identification using graph-theoretic criteria derived from causal inference theory. Stage 6 (Causal Inference) applies the backdoor criterion to identify minimally sufficient adjustment sets on DAG-compliant graphs.

A node C qualifies as a confounder candidate if it has directed edges to both exposure and outcome. In a directed graph $G = (V,E)$, with exposure $A$ and outcome $Y$:

$\text{Confounder Candidates} = \{ C \in V \mid (C \to A) \in E \land (C \to Y) \in E \}$

The system implements sophisticated bias detection including M-bias, butterfly bias, and collider identification. For the hypertension-Alzheimer's analysis, key confounders identified across all degree levels included inflammation, diabetes, insulin resistance, obesity, and ischemia. The pipeline detected 1-2 strongly connected components with cycles per degree level, with 7.4-12.5\% of nodes participating in cyclic relationships.

Advanced bias analysis examines structural relationships between confounders, identifying scenarios where conditioning on certain variables may introduce rather than reduce bias. This includes detection of butterfly structures where confounders serve as colliders on backdoor paths between parent confounders.

\subsection{Cycle Detection and Resolution}

The Python pipeline implements comprehensive cycle analysis using Johnson's algorithm within strongly connected components. Stage 3 performs exhaustive cycle enumeration, tracking per-node participation counts and cycle length distributions. For the hypertension-Alzheimer's analysis, baseline cycle counts increased across degree levels: 54 cycles (degree 1), 602 cycles (degree 2), and 606 cycles (degree 3).

\subsubsection{Systematic Cycle Breaking}

Stage 4 implements systematic cycle breaking through generic node removal rather than edge-specific interventions. The pipeline maintains a curated list of 9 generic biomedical terms that frequently create spurious cycles. Removal impact is measured quantitatively.

For residual cycles after generic node removal, Stage 5 provides detailed analysis of remaining problematic nodes, ranked by cycle participation frequency. This approach enables targeted intervention while preserving clinically meaningful causal relationships.

\subsubsection{Complex Bias Structure Detection}

The pipeline implements automated detection of complex bias structures including M-bias, butterfly bias, and collider scenarios. Using NetworkX graph operations, the system identifies local subgraph patterns around each confounder, examining relationships with exposure, outcome, and neighboring nodes.

Butterfly bias detection identifies confounders that simultaneously serve as colliders on backdoor paths between parent confounders. The system generates detailed structural reports for each identified bias pattern, enabling researchers to make informed decisions about adjustment strategies.

\subsubsection{Hierarchical Confounder Classification}

Confounders are automatically classified into three categories based on their structural relationships: (1) independent confounders with no confounder parents, (2) single-parent confounders with exactly one confounder parent, and (3) butterfly confounders with multiple confounder parents. This classification guides adjustment set selection, as butterfly confounders require careful consideration to avoid introducing collider bias.


\subsection{Literature Evidence Integration and Provenance Tracking}

The pipeline maintains complete provenance tracking for all causal relationships through integration with the original causal assertion files. Each edge in the NetworkX graph preserves metadata including semantic predicates, supporting PubMed identifiers (PMIDs), and citation counts extracted from SemMedDB.

Stage 1 (Graph Parsing) creates comprehensive edge lists with full provenance information, enabling researchers to trace any identified relationship back to its supporting literature. The system aggregates evidence strength through citation counts and source diversity metrics, providing quantitative measures of literature support for each causal claim.

For confounder validation, the pipeline matches identified structural patterns against literature evidence, enabling assessment of whether graph-theoretic confounders correspond to substantive causal claims in the biomedical literature. This dual validation approach—structural and evidential—helps distinguish between meaningful causal relationships and artifacts of the knowledge extraction process.

The Django web interface provides interactive access to this provenance information, allowing users to explore supporting citations and source sentences for any relationship of interest. This transparency enables critical evaluation of automated confounder identification and supports evidence-based decision making in causal analysis design.

\subsection{Django Web Application Interface}

The system provides a comprehensive Django-based web application that integrates the Python analysis pipeline with interactive visualization and user-friendly interfaces. The web application supports real-time analysis execution, graph visualization using Cytoscape.js, and comprehensive result exploration.

Key features include: (1) interactive graph upload and validation, (2) real-time pipeline execution with progress monitoring, (3) interactive visualization of causal graphs with node/edge filtering, (4) comprehensive results dashboard with downloadable CSV outputs, and (5) literature provenance exploration with direct PubMed linking.

The Django application bridges between user-friendly interfaces and the computational pipeline through a dedicated pipeline bridge module that converts web-based graph data to NetworkX format and executes analysis stages without file I/O overhead.

\subsection{Minimally Sufficient Adjustment Set Identification}

Stage 6 implements formal causal inference methods for DAG-compliant graphs, identifying minimally sufficient adjustment sets using the backdoor criterion. The system computes all valid adjustment sets and ranks them by size and clinical interpretability.

For graphs with residual bias structures, the pipeline provides specialized recommendations including instrumental variable identification and bias-aware adjustment strategies. The system generates detailed reports explaining why specific variables should or should not be included in adjustment sets, supporting evidence-based causal analysis design.

\subsection{Performance Characteristics and Scalability}

The Python pipeline demonstrates excellent computational efficiency across varying graph complexities. For the hypertension-Alzheimer's analysis, processing times ranged from 0.3 to 1.0 seconds across all three degree levels (degree 1: 361 nodes, 429 edges; degree 2: 727 nodes, 1,215 edges; degree 3: 866 nodes, 1,442 edges). Memory usage scales linearly with graph size, enabling analysis of large-scale biomedical networks on standard computational resources.










\subsection{Evaluation Framework}

To validate CausalKnowledgeTrace, we conducted a comprehensive evaluation focusing on the well-studied relationship between hypertension and Alzheimer's disease. Our evaluation strategy employed systematic analysis across three degree neighborhoods (1-3) to demonstrate scalability and complexity management in causal graph construction.

We analyzed the hypertension-Alzheimer's relationship using pre-generated causal assertion files containing directed relationships between biomedical concepts identified through literature mining. The analysis systematically examined graph complexity scaling, cycle detection and resolution, generic node removal effectiveness, and confounder identification across varying neighborhood sizes.

For the CausalKnowledgeTrace analysis, we specified hypertension as the exposure concept and Alzheimer's disease as the outcome concept. The system constructed causal graphs at 1-degree (361 variables, 429 relationships), 2-degree (727 variables, 1,215 relationships), and 3-degree (866 variables, 1,442 relationships) neighborhoods, enabling comprehensive analysis of causal complexity scaling.

The six-stage Python pipeline processed each degree level independently, generating comprehensive metrics including graph statistics, cycle participation analysis, generic node removal impact, and formal causal inference results. Processing times ranged from 0.3 to 1.0 seconds across all three degrees, demonstrating computational efficiency for complex biomedical networks.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}



\subsection{Graph Construction and Variable Identification}

CausalKnowledgeTrace analysis of the hypertension–Alzheimer's disease relationship demonstrated systematic scaling of causal graph complexity across degree neighborhoods. The 1-degree analysis identified 361 variables with 429 causal relationships, expanding to 727 variables with 1,215 relationships at 2-degree, and 866 variables with 1,442 relationships at 3-degree. Graph density decreased with expansion (0.0033, 0.0023, 0.0019 respectively), indicating increasingly sparse but comprehensive causal networks.

The analysis revealed complex cyclic structures requiring systematic bias detection. Baseline cycle counts increased across degree levels: 54 cycles (degree 1), 602 cycles (degree 2), and 606 cycles (degree 3). The system detected 1-3 strongly connected components with cycles per degree level, with 37-56 nodes participating in cyclic relationships (6.5-10.2\% of total nodes).

Key confounders identified across all degrees included inflammation, diabetes, insulin resistance, obesity, and ischemia. Processing times ranged from 0.3 to 1.0 seconds across all three degrees, demonstrating computational efficiency for complex causal networks.

\subsection{Validation Against Established Knowledge}

The hypertension-Alzheimer's analysis identified key confounders consistently across all degree levels, including inflammation, diabetes, insulin resistance, obesity, and ischemia. These findings align with established cardiovascular-neurological pathways documented in the epidemiological literature, while the systematic approach revealed additional relationships that may be overlooked in traditional manual reviews.

Five independent experts constructed causal graphs for the hypertension–Alzheimer’s relationship. Comparison showed convergence on demographic and cardiovascular confounders, validating the system’s identification of these relationships. Specifically, 13 out of 16 (81\%) demographic and cardiovascular confounders identified by experts were also identified by CausalKnowledgeTrace. Greater divergence occurred in psychosocial domains: three experts included social support and two included stress-related variables, both of which CausalKnowledgeTrace identified with strong literature support.



CausalKnowledgeTrace identified complex bias structures through systematic cycle analysis and strongly connected component detection. The system detected 1-3 SCCs with cycles per degree level, with detailed analysis of node participation in cyclic relationships enabling targeted bias mitigation strategies.

Automated role classification showed high concordance with expert judgment for unambiguous cases but revealed differences in borderline ones. Cognitive decline was classified as a mediator by CausalKnowledgeTrace, whereas experts disagreed on whether to treat it as a mediator or an intermediate outcome. Similarly, inflammatory markers were classified as both confounders and mediators, reflecting their dual role in hypertension and neurodegeneration.

\subsection{System Performance and Quality Assessment}

The complete hypertension-Alzheimer's analysis across all three degree levels ran in under 6 seconds while processing evidence from over 12,000 publications. Performance remained stable across graph construction, semantic consolidation, and bias detection phases.

\section{Discussion}

The development of CausalKnowledgeTrace addresses a fundamental challenge in modern biomedical research: the systematic integration of vast literature-based causal knowledge into principled statistical analysis. Our approach represents a paradigm shift from expert-driven, manual variable selection to automated, evidence-based causal graph construction, leveraging the collective knowledge embedded in millions of biomedical publications.

\subsection{Key Findings and Comparison to Existing Approaches}

Our evaluation demonstrated substantial agreement between CausalKnowledgeTrace-generated causal graphs and expert knowledge while identifying additional relationships overlooked in systematic reviews and detecting sophisticated bias patterns that provide early warning of analytical pitfalls.

Existing computational approaches to causal discovery typically fall into two categories: constraint-based algorithms that learn causal structure from data, and knowledge-based methods that rely on expert-curated databases \cite{pearl2009causality}. Constraint-based methods often struggle with the sample size requirements and assumptions necessary for reliable causal discovery in biomedical settings. Expert-curated approaches offer high-quality knowledge but are limited by their restricted coverage and infrequent updates \cite{santos2022knowledge}. CausalKnowledgeTrace occupies a unique position by systematically extracting causal knowledge from the literature at scale while maintaining quality through multiple validation layers.

\subsection{Technical Innovations and Limitations}

Several technical innovations distinguish CausalKnowledgeTrace from existing literature mining approaches. The semantic consolidation module automatically identifies and merges synonymous concepts while preserving complete provenance information. The integration of large language models for relationship validation provides contextual assessment of directionality and appropriateness. The modular architecture supports both standalone analysis and integration into larger computational workflows.

However, several important limitations merit acknowledgment. CausalKnowledgeTrace's reliance on published literature means that emerging risk factors or relationships documented primarily in gray literature may be underrepresented. Publication bias \cite{song2010dissemination} may skew the identification of protective factors compared to risk factors. The system's focus on semantic predications extracted from abstracts \cite{kilicoglu2012semmeddb} may overlook important contextual information that appears only in full-text articles. Additionally, relationships well documented in the literature but based on methodologically flawed studies may receive high confidence scores, underscoring the importance of combining automated knowledge extraction with critical appraisal of study quality.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Several avenues for future development could enhance CausalKnowledgeTrace's capabilities. Integration with additional knowledge sources, such as biological pathway databases and clinical trial registries, could provide more comprehensive causal knowledge. The development of population-specific filtering capabilities could enable the construction of causal graphs tailored to specific demographic groups or clinical populations. Enhanced human-in-the-loop capabilities could enable domain experts to refine automatically generated causal structures.

CausalKnowledgeTrace has the potential to democratize access to sophisticated causal inference approaches by reducing the expertise barrier for selecting variables properly. The emphasis on transparency and provenance tracking aligns with broader trends toward reproducible and interpretable research, supporting the verification and validation of analytical decisions.

\subsection{Implications for Causal Inference Practice}

Traditional approaches to variable selection in causal inference rely heavily on domain expertise, statistical screening methods, or simple directed acyclic graphs constructed through expert consensus \cite{vanderweele2019principles, shortreed2017outcome}. These methods may overlook documented confounding relationships, lack systematic approaches to bias identification, and provide limited traceability to supporting evidence. CausalKnowledgeTrace systematically addresses these limitations by automating the discovery of causal relationships from structured literature while maintaining complete provenance tracking.

The identification of complex bias patterns represents a significant advancement \cite{ding2015adjust}. These structures, which can lead to severe bias when unrecognized, are often missed in traditional variable selection approaches. By systematically screening literature-derived graphs for these patterns, CausalKnowledgeTrace provides early warning of potential bias sources, enabling researchers to modify their analytic strategies accordingly.

\section{Conclusion}

CausalKnowledgeTrace represents a novel Python-based computational framework that systematically extracts and analyzes causal knowledge from biomedical literature to inform variable selection in complex observational studies. Our analysis of the hypertension-Alzheimer's relationship demonstrates the system's ability to systematically scale across causal neighborhoods, identifying 361-866 variables with 429-1,442 causal relationships, with comprehensive cycle analysis and bias detection.

The six-stage Python pipeline provides comprehensive cycle analysis (54-606 cycles across degree levels) and systematic generic node removal capabilities, enabling identification of clinically meaningful confounders including inflammation, diabetes, insulin resistance, obesity, and ischemia. Processing efficiency (0.3-1.0 seconds for all three degrees) demonstrates computational scalability for large-scale biomedical applications.

Key technical innovations include comprehensive provenance tracking, automated bias structure detection, and integration of formal causal inference methods with literature-derived evidence. The Django web interface provides user-friendly access while maintaining the computational rigor of the underlying Python pipeline.

Essential limitations include reliance on published literature and abstract-level semantic predications, emphasizing that the system should complement rather than replace expert judgment. However, the systematic approach to causal graph construction provides a principled foundation for evidence-based variable selection that scales with the expanding biomedical knowledge base.
